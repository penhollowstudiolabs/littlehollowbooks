The Mountains Were Never Backwards

On what gets lost when a culture learns to look away from its highlands

There's a particular kind of compliment that reveals more than it intends. "You look so mestiza." "You don't seem like you're from the province." These aren't insults — they're meant kindly. But underneath them sits a hierarchy that's been absorbed so completely it doesn't even feel like a hierarchy anymore: cities as progress, mountains as backwards.

That orientation toward Manila, toward English, toward colonial preference isn't written down anywhere. It shows up instead in corrected accents, in family embarrassment about provincial origins, in the casual, dismissive word "dialect" used for native languages that are, in every linguistic sense, languages of their own.

My wife Kathleen is Ilocano. Even a non-remote province like Ilocos Norte orients outward — toward the capital, toward what counts as modern. Indigenous mountain communities, and the terraced agriculture they've sustained for centuries, are too often presented as historical artifacts: things that were, rather than living sources of wisdom that still are.

"Indigenous" isn't a label that belongs only to a few remote groups — it describes all pre-colonial peoples, Ilocanos included, even though hierarchies have been artificially constructed between groups since then. And that hierarchy connects to something larger: an antiBlackness that runs through colonized cultures worldwide — the specific devaluation of darkness itself as a marker of backwardness.

This is part of why we make bilingual picture books. We want to put Ilocano and English side by side, as equal partners, on the same page. We want mountain wisdom sitting next to modern achievement, not beneath it. These mountains are worth claiming. They were never backwards — we were just taught to look away from them.
